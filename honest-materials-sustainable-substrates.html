The Honest Materials

There is a polite version of this article in which I list every sustainable material on the spec sheets, give it a five-star rating, and call it a guide. This is not that article. I have specified, fabricated and torn down enough installations to have an opinion on most of these materials, and the gap between the spec sheet and what happens in a loading bay at 2 a.m. is wider than it should be.

What follows is a practical comparison of the seven sustainable substrates I keep coming back to in experiential design. For each one I give you the numbers a producer actually needs (weight, span, fire rating, lead time, cost), the failure mode I have personally witnessed, and the end-of-life path the material supports without pretending.

A warning before we start: "sustainable" is doing a lot of work in this industry. A material is only as sustainable as its actual fate after the show. A mycelium panel that ends up in a landfill bin alongside the gaffer tape is not measurably better than MDF; the difference between the two is in the disassembly plan, not in the molecule. I'll come back to this in the Designing for Disassembly piece, but please carry the thought as you read.

How I'm comparing them

For each material I list:

  • Areal weight in kg/m² for the most common build thickness.
  • Practical span — the longest unsupported run I have personally seen hold for the full duration of a one-week event.
  • Reaction-to-fire class under EN 13501-1, the European harmonised standard. B-s1,d0 is the threshold most exhibition halls in the EU/AT will demand for trade fairs; many municipal venues accept C-s2,d0 with sprinklered halls.
  • Indicative price in EUR per square metre, based on quotes I have personally collected in the DACH region in the last 24 months. These are 1-of-100 to 1-of-500 quantities — bulk procurement will go lower.
  • End-of-life path that actually closed the loop on at least one of my projects.

I'll list them roughly in the order I tend to reach for them.

1. Corrugated cardboard (B-flute, double-walled)

The workhorse. I built the LIVING PAPER origami stage at Republic Salzburg in 2014 entirely out of 5 mm B-flute cardboard, hot-glued and stapled. The look held; the disassembly did not.

PropertyValue
Areal weight0.7–1.1 kg/m² (5 mm B-flute), 1.5–2.0 kg/m² (BC double wall)
Practical unsupported span600 mm with reinforcing folds
Fire class (untreated)E (very flammable)
Fire class (treated)B-s1,d0 reachable with phosphate-based intumescent coatings
Indicative price4 to 9 EUR/m² (single-wall), 7 to 14 EUR/m² (double-wall)
Lead time3–10 working days for cut-to-pattern from a regional packaging supplier
End-of-lifePaper stream, contamination-free if you avoid hot-melt glue and PVC-based tapes

The failure mode I keep seeing: Cardboard panels are spec'd by visual designers as if they were plywood, then bolted with M6 hardware that crushes the corrugation under any preload. Use folded triangulation to carry load, never local fasteners through a flat sheet. If you must mechanically fasten, sandwich a 100 × 100 mm hardboard washer on both sides.

Why I still reach for it: When the disposal plan is "into the recycling bin behind the venue", cardboard is the only material on this list that genuinely closes the loop without industrial infrastructure. That has saved my CO₂ accounting on three projects this year alone.

2. Mycelium-grown panels

Mycelium composites — fungal mycelium grown over an agricultural-waste substrate (hemp hurd, sawdust, oat husk) — went from "MoMA installation" to "you can actually order it" somewhere around 2022. I have specified panels from Mogu and from Ecovative's Forager line.

PropertyValue
Areal weight6–10 kg/m² for a 25 mm panel
Practical unsupported span400 mm without backing
Fire classB-s1,d0 (Mogu Acoustic, with internal binder); C-s2,d0 typical for unbacked panels
Indicative price90 to 220 EUR/m² depending on finish
Lead time8 to 16 weeks. Materials are grown to order.
End-of-lifeCompostable, but only home-compostable in the unbacked, untreated variants. Always check the spec sheet.

The thing nobody tells you: Mycelium panels keep growing if humidity exceeds ~70% for any sustained period. I lost a wall section at an outdoor festival in Steiermark because of a single rainy night. Indoor and climate-controlled only, unless the brief explicitly invites the bloom.

Why it earns its price: Acoustic absorption coefficient is genuinely good (αw 0.55 to 0.75 in the variants I've measured) and the surface reads as warm and hand-made on camera. For installations in galleries and high-end retail, it does work that nothing else on this list does.

3. Reclaimed solid timber

Pulled from demolition, harbours, or industrial palette stock. In Austria, BauKarussell is the most reliable yard I have used; for special pieces I have driven to Hamburg to source ship-deck oak.

PropertyValue
Areal weight6–9 kg/m² (per 10 mm thickness) for softwoods
Practical span1.8 m as a 100 × 50 stud, simply supported
Fire classD-s2,d0 untreated; B-s1,d0 with intumescent varnish
Indicative price20 to 80 EUR/m² rough-sawn, double for planed-and-fingerprinted
Lead timeInventory-dependent: today or never
End-of-lifeReuse, then biomass. Genuine downcycling-resistant.

The hidden cost: Reclaimed timber requires inspection for ferrous inclusions (old nails, screws, structural bolts) before any saw work. A metal detector pass on every length added about 15% to my build hours on the last reclaimed project. Budget for it.

4. Bioplastic sheet (PLA, PHA, lignin-blend)

For transparency or specific colourways where mycelium and cardboard cannot deliver, bioplastic sheet is the option I trust. PLA is by far the most available; PHA is much better for end-of-life but still rare and 3–4× the price.

PropertyValue
Areal weight (3 mm)3.7 kg/m² (PLA), 3.5 kg/m² (PHA)
Practical span800 mm for 3 mm sheet, edge-supported
Fire classE typical. Limited fire-rated bioplastic available; route through structural timber sub-frames for Austrian venue codes.
Indicative price25 to 60 EUR/m² (PLA), 90 to 180 EUR/m² (PHA)
Lead time2–4 weeks via specialist distributor
End-of-lifePLA: industrial composting only. PHA: home-compostable in 6–12 months in the variants I've used.

Honest caveat: Most "bioplastic" in event production today is PLA, and PLA needs an industrial composter operating at 58 °C+ to break down on a useful timescale. If your local waste stream doesn't have that infrastructure (most Austrian municipalities do not, as of writing), PLA in a regular bin is functionally identical to fossil plastic in a regular bin. Specify PHA where the budget allows, or specify reuse as the intended end-of-life.

5. Organic-cotton or hemp textile

For draping, projection screens, soft sculpture. The "GOTS-certified organic cotton" (Global Organic Textile Standard) line at most fabric suppliers is honest about its supply chain in a way that "eco" labels often are not.

PropertyValue
Areal weight0.18–0.32 kg/m² (calico to canvas weights)
Fire classE untreated; B-s1,d0 with [Trevira CS-equivalent] FR finish, which is washable to 40 °C
Indicative price8 to 22 EUR/m² for GOTS calico; hemp 18 to 35 EUR/m²
Lead timeIn stock at most contract-textile dealers
End-of-lifeReuse first (most fabric goes into 2–4 shows in my experience). Then textile recycling, then composting if untreated.

The trap: A GOTS-certified base cloth treated with a non-GOTS FR coating is no longer compostable, even though it carries the label on the tag. Specify the FR coating chemistry up front and document it in the material passport.

6. Recycled aluminium honeycomb panel

A composite panel — two thin aluminium skins bonded to an aluminium honeycomb core — produced from post-consumer recycled aluminium. Hexel and Plascore both make recycled-content variants now. Used for stiff, light, structural panels that also need to be flat to within a millimetre.

PropertyValue
Areal weight4.0–5.5 kg/m² for a 15 mm panel
Practical unsupported span1.5 m for a 15 mm panel, supported on two edges
Fire classA2-s1,d0 — meets the strictest exhibition-hall codes I have encountered
Indicative price80 to 140 EUR/m² for recycled-content panels
Lead time4 to 8 weeks
End-of-lifeAluminium recycling stream. Recycled aluminium uses about 5 % of the energy of primary smelting, so the loop is genuinely closed if the panels make it back to a metal recycler — which requires upstream coordination.

Why I love it: It is the one material on this list that I can cantilever, fly from a truss point, and not worry about. The recycled-content premium over standard honeycomb is small (about 12% on my last quote). The catch is that the bond line uses an epoxy that is not separable; the aluminium is recyclable, the assembly is not perfectly so.

7. Hempcrete

Hemp hurds bound with a lime binder, cast in formwork. Almost never used in experiential design, which is exactly why I include it: it can replace gypsum-board partition walls on long-running installations (months, not weeks) with a vapour-open, biogenic alternative that smells faintly of hay.

PropertyValue
Areal weight (75 mm)33 kg/m² — the heavy option on this list
Practical useNon-loadbearing infill walls between a timber stud frame
Fire classB-s1,d0 (depends on supplier; verify per project)
Indicative price55 to 90 EUR/m² for a 75 mm wall, mixed and placed
Lead time2 weeks for materials; 1 day per ~30 m² for casting; 14+ days drying
End-of-lifeCrush and re-cast for the next install, or compost on site

The use case I keep finding: Permanent or semi-permanent retail and museum installations where humidity buffering and acoustic mass actually matter, and where the visitor will be in the space for more than a few minutes. The mass and the smell do work that a gypsum wall does not.

A material-selection cheat sheet

A simplified decision flow I use on first-pass briefs:

  1. Is the show indoor, climate-controlled, and ≤ 30 days? → Cardboard or mycelium.
  2. Does the brief require A2 fire rating or structural cantilevers? → Recycled aluminium honeycomb.
  3. Is the show outdoor, or longer than 30 days indoors? → Reclaimed timber as the structural skeleton, with cardboard, textile, or hemp panel skins.
  4. Is "compostable" written into the brief and the venue actually has industrial compost access? → PLA bioplastic. Otherwise PHA, or pivot to mycelium.
  5. Is the installation permanent or semi-permanent (months)? → Hempcrete on a timber frame.

What this article does not cover

I have left out three materials that frequently turn up on sustainability decks:

  • Recycled PET felt. Excellent acoustic and aesthetic, but it is a downcycle: every thermal cycle shortens the polymer chain. I treat it as one-life material and budget accordingly.
  • Bamboo. Excellent in places where it grows. In central Europe, the carbon footprint of importing it from Asia tends to undo the on-paper benefit. Defensible only if you can source from a European bamboo cultivar (rare).
  • Cork. Wonderful, very specific use cases (tactile floors, acoustic panels). Its supply is concentrated in Portugal and Spain; quality and lead time vary more than the marketing suggests.
