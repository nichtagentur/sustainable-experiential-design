# Five Reuse Patterns

A material is recyclable. A component is reusable. A *system* is what makes that reuse happen at scale. The single most useful framing I've found for this is to stop talking about "sustainable materials" and start talking about reuse patterns — recurring strategies that show up across hundreds of installations, each with different cost, lead-time and aesthetic profiles.

Below are the five patterns I keep seeing succeed. Each section gives the pattern, when to reach for it, when not to, and a candid note on the trade-off you are accepting by choosing it. I have personally worked with all five; the [LIVING PAPER](https://github.com/nichtagentur/living-paper-video) origami stage I built in 2014 used none of them, which is part of why it ended in a skip.

## Pattern 1: Kit-of-Parts

**The pattern.** Design the installation entirely out of standardised, off-the-shelf components — typically scaffold tube and clamp, Octanorm, Aluvision, Layher SpeedyScaff, or rental truss. Custom appearance is achieved by how the parts are arranged and what is attached to them, not by the parts themselves.

**When to reach for it.** Time-constrained briefs. Touring shows. Fairs. Activations where the budget for fabrication is small and the budget for visual impact is large. Whenever the client asks "can we do this six more times in six other cities".

**When not to.** When the visual language explicitly calls for a non-industrial finish — a museum show, a gallery, a hospitality fit-out. Kit-of-parts always reads as a kit-of-parts. Hiding it requires the same labour as bespoke fabrication, at which point you have lost the cost advantage.

**The trade-off.** You give up uniqueness in exchange for a near-zero waste profile. Every component is rented, every component goes back. The only thing you fabricate is the soft elements (graphics, fabric skins, lighting), which is exactly the right place to be fabricating.

**Practical advice.** Standardise on one rental supplier per region and learn their catalogue inside out. The 992 mm Octanorm grid and the 50 × 50 mm aluminium box truss are the two systems I default to in central Europe. A two-hour conversation with a supplier's CAD library — Octanorm publishes one freely, as does Layher — will save you fifty hours of bespoke detailing.

## Pattern 2: Harvest-from-Deconstruction

**The pattern.** Source the structural and finish materials from a building or installation that is being deconstructed. The reclaimed-timber yards I covered in [The Honest Materials](./01-honest-materials.html) are one node in this network; municipal "Materialbörse" projects, demolition contractors with selective deconstruction practice, and the rising number of "harvest map" platforms (Concular in Germany, MateriO in France) are the others.

**When to reach for it.** When the brief tolerates — or actively wants — visible signs of previous life: timber with old fastener marks, brick with mortar residue, sheet metal with weld scars. When the budget allows for the inspection time the previous article warned about. When the project is in a region with active deconstruction yards (which excludes large parts of Europe, including most of rural Austria).

**When not to.** Tight schedules. Trade-fair briefs that demand sterile finishes. Anything where the material has to pass aseptic or food-contact requirements, which the harvest stream cannot guarantee.

**The trade-off.** You accept variability. A harvest yard does not have 80 m² of any one timber stock; you build with what is there or you wait. This is the single biggest reason designers shy away from this pattern, and it is also the single biggest reason the projects that do commit to it look unlike any other project on the floor.

**Practical advice.** Build the design around a single anchor material that you have already physically inspected at the yard. Do not specify a harvested material in CAD that you have not held in your hand. The design language extrapolates from the anchor; the rest of the materials become subordinate. This is the inverse of the trade-fair workflow and it is uncomfortable for designers trained on it.

## Pattern 3: Borrowed-Not-Bought

**The pattern.** Specify components that are owned by an inventory house — props, furniture, soft goods, lighting, sometimes entire stage architectures — and rented to your project for the duration of the show. The component goes back into the inventory at the end and is rented again to the next project.

**When to reach for it.** Whenever the component is specifiable from a prop house's catalogue. Furniture is the obvious case (Vondom, Festival Group, and on the higher end Vitra's rental programme); lighting and rigging are universally rental in the live-event industry already; what is less obvious is the rental availability of art props, signage hardware, and even custom-built scenic elements through dedicated set-and-prop houses.

**When not to.** When the component must be branded, signed, or otherwise customised in a way the rental house cannot reverse. (You can have something repainted at end of run, but the cost equation often defeats the rental.) When the rental house's inventory is geographically too far for the project budget to bear the shipping.

**The trade-off.** You pay for the optionality. A piece of furniture you rent for ten days will cost you 25-40% of its purchase price; rented twice it has cost you the whole asset. Where this still pays is the storage and disposal cost you avoid: not having to warehouse the chairs between shows is, in expensive European urban inventory, often the bigger saving.

**Practical advice.** Treat rental availability as a *hard constraint at concept stage*, not a procurement decision at production stage. Two design-time decisions kill rentability: a non-standard dimension that nobody owns, and a custom finish that prevents return. Avoid both at concept and the rental option stays open.

## Pattern 4: Pre-Loved Skins

**The pattern.** Re-use the soft surface of a previous installation — printed banner fabric, projection scrim, vinyl wraps, even painted cardboard — by re-cutting, over-printing, or simply re-using as-is, in a subsequent show. The skin carries with it visible evidence of its previous life: trim cuts, faded seams, the faint imprint of the previous show's typography under fresh ink.

**When to reach for it.** Festival contexts, public-art installations, anything with a "circular" narrative the client wants to make visible. Brands that are honest about their second-life programmes (Patagonia, Freitag, Vetrai of Murano, increasingly the larger sportswear and outdoor brands) are receptive to skins that show their re-use rather than hiding it.

**When not to.** Premium luxury, automotive launch, anything where fingerprints — literal or figurative — are forbidden. The aesthetic of the pre-loved skin is incompatible with the aesthetic of the freshly-unwrapped product launch.

**The trade-off.** You accept that the new piece will inherit some visual property from the old one. This can be a creative gift (a beautiful palimpsest) or a creative obstacle (a faded purple graphic underneath your new white space). The skill in this pattern is reading the previous piece for what it gives you, not fighting against it.

**Practical advice.** Build a small in-house fabric library. After every show, the pieces of fabric over 1 m² get rolled, tagged with their dimensions, weight, FR-treatment status, and any visible features, and stored in a stacker rack. After two or three shows you have an inventory worth specifying *into* — you design the next show partly around what is on the rack. This single piece of operational discipline is the difference between aspiring to circular reuse and actually doing it.

## Pattern 5: Grown Materials

**The pattern.** Specify a material that is biologically grown for the project — mycelium panels, kombucha leather, algae-based bioplastics, living moss walls, lichen culture — and either compost it on-site at strike or hand it to a horticultural partner who continues to grow it after the show.

**When to reach for it.** Long lead-time projects (eight weeks and up) where the brief explicitly calls for a "material innovation" story. Projects where the educational or narrative value of "this was alive" pulls weight with the audience. Climate-controlled venues where the grown material can be kept in its viable temperature and humidity window.

**When not to.** Short turnarounds. Anything outdoors and exposed to weather. Anything where the venue cannot guarantee 18-22 °C and 40-60% RH for the duration of the show. Anything with significant transport between fabrication and venue, especially across border-controls that may classify the material as agricultural goods.

**The trade-off.** You accept that the material is partly out of your control. It will not be a uniform colour. It may continue to grow during the show. It will smell faintly of the substrate it was grown on (oat husk smells like oats; hemp hurd smells faintly of hay). For some projects this is the entire point; for others it is a disqualifying property.

**Practical advice.** Specify two months earlier than you would for any other material, and budget for a 10% mortality rate — at least one panel will arrive damaged or partly grown wrong, and the supplier needs to grow you replacements. Order them at the same time as the originals, not after the originals fail.

## Choosing between the patterns

A simple matrix I use at concept stage. Score the brief on each axis from 1 (low) to 5 (high), then read off the patterns that fit:

| Brief property                              | Kit-of-parts | Harvest | Rental | Pre-loved | Grown |
|---------------------------------------------|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|
| Time pressure (high score = tight)          | ✓ |   | ✓ | ✓ |   |
| Bespoke aesthetic required (high)           |   | ✓ |   | ✓ | ✓ |
| Touring or repeatable (high)                | ✓ |   | ✓ | ✓ |   |
| Budget for fabrication labour (high)        |   | ✓ |   | ✓ | ✓ |
| Climate-controlled venue (high)             | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Outdoor or weather-exposed (high)           |   | ✓ | ✓ |   |   |
| Storyworthiness of materiality (high)       |   | ✓ |   | ✓ | ✓ |

Most projects use two or three of these patterns simultaneously. A typical brief I'll deliver this year might be: kit-of-parts for the structural skeleton (rented Octanorm), harvest-from-deconstruction for the cladding (reclaimed pine from a Vienna Hofgang), and grown materials for the centrepiece (a 2.5 × 2.5 m mycelium acoustic shape, four-month grow lead time). Each pattern is doing what it is good at; together they account for 92% of the project's mass and >95% of its visual surface.

## What none of these patterns will do for you

A reuse pattern is necessary but not sufficient. None of them will save a project that has been spec'd with hot-melt glue, with non-modular dimensions, or without a documented end-of-life path. The patterns are the *what* of circular experiential design; the [Designing for Disassembly workflow](./02-designing-for-disassembly.html) is the *how*. Used together, they get you to a result where the show ends and the materials don't.
