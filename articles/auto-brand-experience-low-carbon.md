# Measuring and Reducing the Carbon Footprint of Brand Activations

The allure of a spectacular brand activation is undeniable. We chase that "wow" factor, the immersive experience that lodges itself in the public consciousness. For years, my own work, particularly with projects like the LIVING PAPER 2014 origami stage at Republic Salzburg, was driven by this pursuit of the extraordinary. But that stage, a magnificent but ultimately disposable structure held together with copious amounts of hot-melt glue, ended its short life in a skip. It was a stark, material lesson in the unintended consequences of ephemeral spectacle.

Today, the conversation has shifted. The "wow" must now be accompanied by a "how" – specifically, how do we create these impactful experiences with a lighter touch on our planet? Measuring and reducing the carbon footprint of brand activations isn't just good practice; it's becoming a non-negotiable element of responsible design.

## The Carbon Audit: Where Do We Start?

Before we can reduce, we must understand. Quantifying the carbon footprint of an activation is the crucial first step. This isn't about abstract figures; it's about tangible emissions tied to every decision, from material sourcing to energy consumption and waste disposal.

My team at Papertown begins by breaking down an activation into its core components:

*   **Materials:** This is often the largest contributor. We look at the embodied carbon of every material used – the energy expended in extraction, manufacturing, and transportation. For instance, virgin plastics or metals carry a significantly higher footprint than recycled alternatives or rapidly renewable resources.
*   **Energy:** Powering lighting, AV equipment, digital displays, and even heating or cooling a space all contribute. We assess the source of this energy – is it from a renewable grid, or fossil fuels?
*   **Transportation:** Getting materials to site, transporting the activation components, and the travel of staff and attendees all add up.
*   **Waste:** What happens at the end of the activation? Landfill waste has a considerable carbon cost. Designing for disassembly and reuse directly impacts this.

Tools and methodologies for this are evolving. While a full Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) might be overkill for smaller activations, a simplified carbon accounting approach, similar to those outlined by resources like Scope Carbon Tracking, can provide invaluable insights. We aim for a rough estimate, perhaps within a 10-15% margin of error, focusing on the biggest emission sources. For a medium-sized activation, say 100m², using predominantly virgin materials and standard energy sources, I'd estimate the footprint could easily range from 500 kg to over 2 tonnes of CO2e.

## Material Choices: The Foundation of Low-Carbon Design

The most impactful lever we have is material selection. The "look" of an activation often dictates material choices, but this doesn't have to mean sacrificing sustainability.

*   **Embrace Recycled and Reclaimed:** Cardboard, paper, and wood are obvious candidates, especially when sourced from certified recycled streams. My LIVING PAPER concept, while not always perfectly executed in its early days, was an early exploration into the potential of paper-based structures. Today, we use high-strength recycled cardboard for structural elements, often achieving a density of 300-500 g/m² for key components. The cost difference compared to virgin materials can be negligible, and the carbon saving significant – potentially reducing material footprint by 50-70%.
*   **Prioritise Renewable Resources:** Bamboo, cork, and sustainably harvested timber offer excellent alternatives. Their growth sequesters carbon, and they are often biodegradable.
*   **Consider Reusability:** Designing elements that can be easily dismantled and stored for future use is paramount. This shifts the focus from a single-use object to an asset. Think modular systems, interlocking components, and avoiding permanent adhesives where possible. The Ellen MacArthur Foundation's Circular Design Guide provides excellent frameworks for this.
*   **The "Look" Factor:** A beautiful, high-end finish can be achieved with sustainable materials. For example, high-quality recycled paper stocks can be printed, embossed, or treated to mimic more expensive finishes. Natural wood can be stained or sealed to achieve a polished look. The key is in the craftsmanship and the application of finishes. We've found that a well-executed paint finish on recycled MDF can be indistinguishable from virgin material, with a fraction of the embodied carbon.

## Energy and Operations: Beyond the Static Build

Once the physical structure is considered, we turn to operational aspects.

*   **Efficient Lighting:** LED technology is standard now, but we push for dynamic lighting systems that can be dimmed or turned off when not needed. Exploring projection mapping onto surfaces can create dynamic visual effects with less physical material and energy.
*   **Smart Power Management:** Working with event organisers to ensure power is supplied from renewable sources is crucial. Where this isn't possible, optimising usage – turning off equipment during downtime, using timers – becomes essential.
*   **Waste Management Hierarchy:** Prioritise reduction, then reuse, then recycling. For unavoidable waste, explore composting or energy recovery options over landfill.

## The Cost of Sustainability: A New Equation

The perception that sustainable materials are inherently more expensive is often a misconception. While some cutting-edge bio-materials might carry a premium, many readily available sustainable options are cost-competitive, and often cheaper, than their virgin counterparts.

*   **Material Costs:** Recycled cardboard might cost EUR 0.50-1.50/m² depending on thickness and grade, comparable to or less than many virgin boards. Sustainably sourced timber can range from EUR 300-800/m³ depending on species and grade.
*   **Labour Costs:** Designing for disassembly can sometimes increase initial build time, but significantly reduces de-rig and waste disposal costs. A typical de-rig that involves breaking down and landfilling might cost EUR 500-1000. A de-rig focused on disassembly and storage could cost EUR 700-1200 initially, but yields reusable components and drastically reduced waste fees.
*   **Long-Term Value:** Reusable components represent a capital investment that pays dividends over multiple activations, reducing the need for new material purchases each time.

## Honesty in Design

My LIVING PAPER 2014 stage was a learning experience. It taught me that spectacle without consideration for its end-of-life is irresponsible. Today, the challenge for experiential designers is to create breathtaking, memorable brand experiences that are also deeply considered in their environmental impact. This requires a shift in mindset, a willingness to measure, and a commitment to innovative material and operational choices. The "look" doesn't need to suffer; in fact, the honesty and integrity of a truly sustainable design can often elevate the experience to a new level.
